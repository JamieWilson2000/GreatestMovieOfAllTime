The History of Aesops Fables

Aesop, the slave and storyteller is believed to have been alive around 620 and 564 BCE and living in ancient Greece. He was known to transform situations represented by animals into feasible life lessons

This detail of the 13th-century Fontana Maggiore in Perugia, Italy, with the fables of The Wolf and the Crane and The Wolf and the Lamb show how respected Aesops stories have been throughout the centuries. With origins stretching as far afield as India, philosophers have been left wondering had the fables reached out across continents or was Aesop inspired by stories told by fellow slaves, sold in from foreign lands.

The fable of the farmer and his sons from Caxton's edition, 1484

The first printed version of Aesop's Fables in English was published on 26 March 1484, by William Caxton. Many others were to follow over the centuries. Until the 18th century the fables were largely put to adult use by teachers, preachers, speech-makers and moralists. It was the philosopher John Locke who first seemed to have advocated targeting children as a special audience in "Some Thoughts Concerning Education" (1693).
